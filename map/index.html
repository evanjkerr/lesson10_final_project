FORECLOSURES

Over the last 70 years in the United States, most legacy cities have been experiencing significant decreases in population. There are many reasons as to why this is the case, and many theories as to the permanent damages this trend surely caused. But with these population decreases (as well as economic output decreases) those who were left inherited dire circumstances.

During this time there was a concerted, socially accepted, governmental effort to destroy much of the housing (single family and multi-family) within these neighborhoods to widen roads, store cars, build the national highway system, to build single-use developments and seperate uses, and to have more green space. Ensuing these efforts was an entrenched and destructive cycle of poverty, property depreciation, joblessness, obesity, familial destruction, overcrowding, crime, and drugs. Foreclosures, evictions, and homelessness are all symptoms of a larger historic urban disinvestment trend (and a federally subsidized suburban, car-dependent lifestyle that remains an unquestioned and largely misunderstood part of modern American society) that has been happening in the United States since the end of the Great Depression but grew rapidly after the end of the American-Korean war.

US Housing prices are now the highest they have ever been and there is no end in sight.

US Housing Prices Post-1970.

Modern US housing policy and the homelessness crisis are not as simple as many wish it to be. They are historically contingent, contextually derived phenomena that require rigorous study, historical competency, and some willful cultural distancing.
