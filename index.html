FORECLOSURES

Over the last 70 years in the United States, most legacy cities have been experiencing significant decreases in population. There are many reasons as to why this is the case, and many theories as to the permanent damages this trend has surely caused. But with these population decreases (as well as economic output decreases) those who remained in their urban neighborhoods often inherited dire circumstances.

During this time period there was a concerted, socially accepted, governmental effort to destroy much of the housing (single family and multi-family) within these neighborhoods to widen roads, store cars, build a national highway system, construct single-use developments with seperated uses, and to have more "green space". Ensuing these efforts was an entrenched and destructive cycle of poverty, property depreciation, joblessness, obesity, familial seperation, overcrowding, over-policing, over-incarcerating, crime, and drugs.

Foreclosures, evictions, and homelessness are all symptoms of a larger historic urban disinvestment trend (and a federally subsidized suburban, car-dependent lifestyle that remains an unquestioned and largely misunderstood part of modern American society) that has been advancing in the United States since the end of the Great Depression and grew rapidly in the years after the end of America's involvment in the Korean war.

Modern US housing policy and the homelessness crisis are not as simple as many wish it to be. They are historically contingent, contextually derived phenomena that require rigorous study, historical competency, and some willful cultural distancing.
